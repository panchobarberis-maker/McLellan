Trust & Probate FAQs

Probate is the court-supervised legal process of validating a deceased person's will, paying their debts, and distributing their remaining assets to beneficiaries or heirs. In California, probate is required for estates with assets exceeding $184,500 (as of 2024) that are not held in a trust, joint tenancy, or beneficiary-designated account. The process typically takes 9 months to 2 years and involves filing in the Superior Court of the county where the decedent lived.

A trust is a legal arrangement in which one party (the grantor or settlor) transfers assets to a trustee, who holds and manages those assets for the benefit of one or more beneficiaries. California revocable living trusts allow the grantor to retain control during their lifetime and avoid probate at death. Irrevocable trusts cannot be modified and offer asset protection and tax benefits but require the grantor to relinquish control.

A trustee is the person or institution legally responsible for administering a trust in accordance with its terms and California law. The trustee holds legal title to trust assets and must manage them solely in the interest of the beneficiaries. A trustee can be an individual (often a family member) or a corporate trustee such as a bank or trust company. When the original trustee dies or becomes incapacitated, a successor trustee named in the trust takes over.

Under California Probate Code § 16000 et seq., a trustee owes the beneficiaries strict fiduciary duties including: the duty of loyalty (act solely in the beneficiaries' interest), the duty of prudence (invest and manage assets as a prudent investor would), the duty to account (provide regular accountings to beneficiaries), the duty of impartiality (balance the interests of current and remainder beneficiaries), and the duty to avoid self-dealing. Violating any of these duties can expose the trustee to personal liability for damages and grounds for removal.

Under California Probate Code § 15642, a court may remove a trustee for several reasons: breach of the trust, insolvency or financial unsuitability, failure to act, conviction of a felony, gross misconduct or mismanagement, inability to cooperate with co-trustees in a way that harms the beneficiaries, or where removal is in the best interest of the beneficiaries. A beneficiary, co-trustee, or settlor (if living) can petition the probate court for removal. Courts may also suspend a trustee and appoint a temporary trustee while removal proceedings are pending.

To contest a will in California, you must file a petition in probate court before the will is admitted to probate — or within 120 days after the probate court clerk mails notice to the contestant. Grounds for a will contest include: lack of testamentary capacity (the decedent did not understand what they were signing), undue influence (someone pressured the decedent), fraud, forgery, or failure to comply with California's will execution requirements (Probate Code § 6110). Standing to contest is generally limited to interested parties — those who would inherit under a prior will or the laws of intestate succession. If successful, a will contest can void the contested will entirely or as to specific provisions.

Financial elder abuse in California occurs when someone takes, secretes, appropriates, or retains the property of an elder (age 65 or older) or dependent adult for a wrongful use, with the intent to defraud, or by undue influence. It is governed by the California Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welfare & Institutions Code § 15600 et seq.). Victims or their successors can bring a civil lawsuit for financial recovery of the stolen property, attorney's fees, and enhanced remedies including punitive damages. Common forms include misappropriation of trust assets, fraud by a caregiver or family member, and undue influence over estate planning documents.

In California, a conservatorship is a court-supervised arrangement for adults (18 and older) who lack the capacity to manage their own personal care or financial affairs. A guardianship applies to minors (under 18) who need someone to manage their care or estate. A conservatorship of the person gives the conservator authority over the individual's personal decisions — including residence, medical care, and daily activities. A conservatorship of the estate gives authority over the conservatee's financial assets. Both require court approval and ongoing oversight, including annual accountings and status reports to the probate court.
