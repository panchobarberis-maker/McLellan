Hold Employers Accountable Under PAGA

California's Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA) empowers employees to sue their employers on behalf of themselves, fellow employees, and the State of California to recover civil penalties for Labor Code violations. PAGA claims are powerful tools for enforcing wage and hour laws and can be brought even when the individual dollar amounts at stake would not justify a single-plaintiff lawsuit.

PAGA Claims FAQs

PAGA — the Private Attorneys General Act — allows individual employees to file lawsuits on behalf of themselves and other current and former employees to recover civil penalties for California Labor Code violations. The employee acts as a private attorney general on behalf of the state.

Common PAGA violations include unpaid overtime, meal and rest break violations, failure to provide accurate wage statements, failure to pay all wages at termination, and other violations of the California Labor Code that are subject to civil penalties.

Under PAGA, 75% of civil penalties go to the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA) and 25% is distributed to the affected employees. The employee plaintiff also typically recovers attorney's fees if the claim is successful.

Before filing a PAGA lawsuit, the employee must provide written notice to the employer and to the LWDA describing the specific Labor Code violations. The LWDA has 65 business days to investigate. If it declines to investigate, the employee may proceed with the lawsuit.

PAGA claims must be filed within one year of the last Labor Code violation. Because the notice-and-cure period and LWDA review process take time, it is important to consult an attorney as soon as possible to avoid missing the deadline.
