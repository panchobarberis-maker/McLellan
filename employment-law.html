Yes — and this is one of the biggest misconceptions California employers have. California is an at-will state, which means you can generally terminate an employee at any time, for any reason, or for no reason. But there are critical exceptions:

  • Terminations that violate public policy (e.g., firing someone for filing a workers' compensation claim or taking protected leave)
  • Decisions based on a protected characteristic under FEHA — race, gender, age, disability, religion, sexual orientation, or national origin
  • Retaliation for protected activity such as reporting harassment or filing a wage complaint
  • Breach of an implied contract created by your handbook, offer letter, or statements made during hiring

At-will employment is a starting point, not a shield. Every termination should be documented, reviewed for risk, and handled consistently with your policies. If you are considering a termination and have any concerns, contact McLellan Law Group, LLP before taking action.

A class action requires court certification — a significant procedural hurdle that, if denied, can stop the case from proceeding on a class-wide basis. A PAGA claim works very differently: the employee acts on behalf of the State of California, not a group of individuals, and there is no class certification requirement.

  • PAGA penalties are calculated per employee, per pay period — exposure can be substantial even for technical violations
  • PAGA claims cannot be fully eliminated through arbitration agreements the way individual claims often can
  • 75% of PAGA penalties go to the State; 25% go to the employees
  • The 2024 PAGA reforms introduced new opportunities to cure violations and limit penalties — making early legal strategy critical

A severance agreement is a binding contract — if improperly drafted, it may be unenforceable, meaning you paid severance without getting the protection you expected.

Key legal requirements in California include:

  • For employees 40 or older, the Older Workers Benefit Protection Act requires specific language, a 21-day consideration period (45 days in a group layoff), and a 7-day revocation period
  • A proper Civil Code §1542 waiver is required to release unknown claims
  • Non-disparagement and confidentiality clauses must comply with recent California law changes
  • Certain provisions — like language preventing employees from discussing unlawful workplace conduct — simply cannot be included
