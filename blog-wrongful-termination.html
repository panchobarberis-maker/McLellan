Wrongful Termination in California: What Employees and Employers Need to Know

By Steven McLellan | June 2025 | 10 min read

California operates under the doctrine of at-will employment — meaning either party can terminate the employment relationship at any time, for any reason, or for no reason at all. But that principle has significant legal exceptions, and navigating them is where disputes arise.

For employees, understanding these exceptions can mean the difference between accepting an unjust termination and recovering substantial damages. For employers, awareness of these protections is essential to avoiding costly litigation and reputational harm.

"At-will employment is the rule — but the exceptions are numerous, well-established, and frequently litigated in California courts."

What Is At-Will Employment?

Under California Labor Code § 2922, employment with no specified term is presumed to be at-will. This means an employer can fire an employee without warning and without cause — and an employee can quit at any time. The agreement can be modified by a written employment contract, union agreement, or company policy.

However, the at-will presumption does not give employers a license to terminate employees for illegal reasons.

When a Termination Becomes "Wrongful"

California law recognizes wrongful termination claims in several key circumstances:

1. Discrimination Under FEHA

The California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) prohibits termination based on protected characteristics including:

  • Race, color, national origin, or ancestry
  • Sex, gender identity, or sexual orientation
  • Religion or creed
  • Age (40 and older)
  • Disability (physical or mental)
  • Pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions
  • Marital status or familial status
  • Military or veteran status

Employers with five or more employees are covered by FEHA. A termination that is motivated — even in part — by a protected characteristic can give rise to a discrimination claim.

2. Retaliation

California law broadly protects employees from retaliation for engaging in legally protected activities. Common protected activities include:

  • Reporting workplace safety violations to Cal/OSHA
  • Filing or threatening to file a wage and hour complaint
  • Reporting harassment or discrimination
  • Requesting protected leave under CFRA or FMLA
  • Whistleblowing under Labor Code § 1102.5
  • Participating in an investigation or legal proceeding

Retaliation claims often turn on the timing of the termination relative to the protected activity, and whether the employer's stated reason for termination holds up to scrutiny.

3. Violation of Public Policy (Tameny Claims)

Under the California Supreme Court's ruling in Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980), employees may bring a tort claim for termination that violates a fundamental public policy. Classic examples include firing an employee for:

  • Refusing to commit an illegal act at an employer's direction
  • Exercising a legal right, such as voting or serving on jury duty
  • Reporting employer misconduct to a government agency

These claims are significant because they can support claims for emotional distress damages and punitive damages — beyond what a contract-based claim would allow.

4. Breach of an Implied Employment Contract

Even without a written employment contract, California courts have found that an implied contract of continued employment can arise from an employer's policies, practices, or representations. Evidence may include:

  • An employee handbook promising termination only "for cause"
  • Longevity of employment and consistent positive performance reviews
  • Oral representations by supervisors about job security

If an employer terminates an employee in breach of such an implied contract, the employee may pursue a breach of contract claim even in the absence of a written agreement.

Damages in Wrongful Termination Cases

Remedies available in a California wrongful termination case may include:

  • Lost wages and benefits — past and future earnings the employee would have earned but for the termination
  • Emotional distress damages — in tort-based claims such as Tameny claims or discrimination claims
  • Punitive damages — where the employer's conduct is shown to be malicious, oppressive, or fraudulent
  • Attorney's fees — available under FEHA where the employee prevails
  • Reinstatement — though rarely ordered in practice

What Employers Should Do

Employers can significantly reduce their exposure to wrongful termination claims by implementing and following sound HR practices:

  • Document performance issues consistently and contemporaneously
  • Apply disciplinary policies uniformly across employees
  • Conduct internal investigations before terminating employees who have recently engaged in protected activity
  • Review employee handbooks regularly to ensure policies reflect current law and practice
  • Consult employment counsel before making high-risk termination decisions

What Employees Should Do

If you believe you have been wrongfully terminated, acting promptly is critical. Under FEHA, employees generally must file an administrative complaint with the California Civil Rights Department (CRD) within three years of the alleged violation before filing suit. Other claims have different statutes of limitations.

You should:

  • Preserve all documents, emails, and records relating to your employment and termination
  • Write down the details of relevant conversations while your memory is fresh
  • Avoid signing any severance agreement without consulting an attorney
  • Contact an employment attorney promptly to evaluate your claims
"Wrongful termination cases are often won or lost on documentation. The strength of your case depends on what evidence exists — and when you start preserving it."
