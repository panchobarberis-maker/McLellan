Did Your Employer Fail to Pay You?

California has some of the most comprehensive wage and hour laws in the United States. Employers who fail to pay overtime, misclassify employees as exempt or as independent contractors, or withhold final wages may owe you significant penalties in addition to the unpaid wages themselves.

Unpaid Wages & Overtime FAQs

California law requires overtime pay at 1.5x the regular rate for hours worked over 8 in a day or 40 in a week, and 2x the regular rate for hours over 12 in a day or over 8 on the seventh consecutive day of work in a workweek.

If an employer willfully fails to pay all final wages on time after termination or resignation, California Labor Code § 203 entitles the employee to waiting time penalties equal to one day of wages for each day of delay, up to 30 days.

Not if the classification is incorrect. California has strict criteria for exempt status. If you perform mostly non-exempt duties or don't meet the salary threshold, you may be entitled to overtime regardless of your job title.

The statute of limitations for unpaid wage claims in California is generally three years for Labor Code violations and four years for claims under the Unfair Competition Law (UCL). Contact an attorney promptly to evaluate your options.
